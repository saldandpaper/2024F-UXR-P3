Data Analysis

→We used two main methods to collect data: surveys and interviews.

1.Surveys:

The survey was shared on platforms like Discord, social media (Facebook and Instagram), and personal networks.

A total of 20 people completed the survey, giving us a mix of quantitative data (like YouTube usage frequency) and qualitative insights through open-ended questions.

2.Interviews:

We conducted three semi-structured interviews with participants who provided more detailed feedback.

These interviews focused on their YouTube habits, challenges with video playback, and thoughts on an audio-only feature.

→To analyze the data collected, we used a combination of quantitative and qualitative techniques:

1.Quantitative Analysis:

Frequency Distribution: We analyzed survey responses to identify patterns in YouTube usage, interest in an audio-only feature, and playback challenges.

Data Visualization: We created charts like pie charts and bar graphs to present trends, such as the percentage of daily YouTube users and the level of interest in an audio-only feature.

Cross-Tabulation: We compared demographic data (like age) with responses to understand how different groups perceive the audio-only feature.

2.Qualitative Analysis:

Thematic Analysis: We reviewed open-ended survey responses and interview transcripts to identify recurring themes, such as data-saving needs, multitasking preferences, and buffering frustrations.

Keyword Frequency: Common terms like “data,” “audio,” and “playback” were highlighted to determine what users prioritized.

Synthesis of Insights: We combined the findings from both quantitative and qualitative analysis to create a comprehensive picture. For example, high interest in audio-only features from survey responses was supported by interview feedback emphasizing the need for better playback options during multitasking.

→Summary of Quantitative and Qualitative Data Collected

Quantitative Data

1.YouTube Usage Frequency:

-45% of participants use YouTube daily.

-20% use it several times a week, while others use it less frequently.

2.Interest in an Audio-Only Feature:

-40% of participants said they would “definitely” like an audio-only feature, and 20% were “maybe” interested.

-40% were “very likely” to use such a feature, with an additional 20% being “likely.”

3.Internet Speed and Playback Issues:

-30% reported excellent internet speed with rare playback disruptions.

-40% experienced occasional buffering, and 20% reported frequent interruptions due to poor internet.

4.Knowledge of Third-Party Mirrors:

-40% of participants were aware of third-party tools for audio-only playback, while 60% were not.

Qualitative Data

1.Pain Points and Challenges:

-Participants emphasized issues with high data usage and battery drain when using YouTube for audio.

-Buffering during video playback was a common frustration, particularly for users with poor internet.

2.User Scenarios:

-Many participants highlighted multitasking as a key reason for wanting an audio-only feature (e.g., working, commuting, or exercising).

3.Suggestions and Feedback:

-Positive comments included, “Would love an audio-only mode for music!” and “An audio-only option would be a game-changer for commutes.”

-A small minority expressed doubts, with one participant stating, “I prefer video, so I wouldn’t use it.”

By combining these quantitative trends with qualitative feedback, we gained a clear understanding of user preferences and challenges, reinforcing the demand for an audio-only feature.

Data Analysis: See Reporting Considerations (All Methods)

1. Ask Critical Questions

Patterns and Trends:

Key recurring themes, such as a strong interest in an audio-only feature and frequent pain points like data usage and battery drain, were identified across survey and interview data.

        Example: 40% of participants indicated they would “definitely” use an audio-only feature.

Anomalies and Surprises:

Some participants preferred third-party audio-only solutions despite YouTube’s popularity, highlighting a gap in the platform’s offerings.

        Example: “I already use a third-party app for audio; YouTube should include this natively.”

Alignment with Objectives:

The findings confirmed the research goal: identifying user needs for an audio-only feature while revealing practical challenges such as buffering and multitasking inefficiencies.

2. Structure Your Analysis

Summary of Findings:

The analysis began with an overview of YouTube usage frequency, pain points, and interest in audio-only functionality.

        Example: 45% of participants use YouTube daily, demonstrating its integration into their routines.

Detailed Insights:

Findings were grouped into actionable categories, such as:

        -Data and battery-saving needs.

        -Scenarios where multitasking is a priority.

Contrasting Perspectives:

While the majority supported the feature, a minority indicated they use YouTube primarily for visual content, showing the need for this feature to remain optional.

3.Use of Visualizations

Graphical Representation:

Bar graphs and pie charts were used to highlight trends, such as the percentage of users interested in the feature and the frequency of YouTube usage.

Affinity Mapping:

Tools like Miro were used to organize qualitative data into clusters, such as “Data Efficiency” and “Multitasking Scenarios,” allowing for clearer synthesis.

4. Actionable Insights

-Each finding was translated into clear actions for stakeholders:

        Example: Introduce an audio-only toggle to address data consumption and battery life concerns.

-Insights were prioritized based on their potential impact on user experience and feasibility of implementation.

Prioritize Recommendations:

-Categorize insights by feasibility and impact. For example, “Adding an audio-only mode” could have high impact and be feasible in the short term.

5. Acknowledge Limitations

-The study involved 20 participants, which may limit the generalizability of findings.

-Participants were primarily recruited from tech-savvy platforms, which may bias responses toward users already familiar with YouTube alternatives.
